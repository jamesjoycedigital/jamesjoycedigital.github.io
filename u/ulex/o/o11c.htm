The summer evening had begun to fold all nature in its deep mysterious warmth. Faraway in the west the sun was setting and the last glow of all too fleeting day lingered softly upon sea and strand, on the proud promontory of dear old faithful Howth, guarding as ever the waters of the bay, on the old weedgrown rocks along Sandymount shore and, last but not least, on the quiet church whence streamed forth at times upon the stillness music of prayer to her who is, in her pure radiance, a beacon ever to the stormtossed heart of man, Mary, star of the sea.

The three girl friends were seated on the rocks, enjoying the evening air which was fresh but not too chilly. Many a time & oft would they come there to their favourite nook to have a cosy chat & discuss things feminine Cissy Caffrey and and Edie Boardman with the baby boy in the pushcar and the Tommy and Jacky Caffrey, two little curlyheaded boys, dressed in sailor suits with caps to match and the name H. M. S. Belleisle on both. For Tommy and Jacky were twins, scarce four years old, and very noisy and spoiled twins sometimes but for all that darling little fellows with bright merry faces and endearing ways about them. They were dabbling with their spades and buckets in the sand, building castles as little children do, or playing with their big coloured ball, as happy as the day was long. But who was Gertie?

Gertie MacDowell who was seated near her companions lost in thought, gazing far away into the distance was in very truth as fair a specimen of winsome Irish girlhood as one could wish to see. She was pronounced beautiful by all who knew her though, as folks often said, she was more a Giltrap than a MacDowell. Her figure was slight and graceful, inclining even to fragility, but

The waxen pallor of her face was almost spiritual in its ivorylike purity. Her hands were of finely veined alabaster, with tapering fingers and as white as lemonjuice and queen of ointments could make them though it was not true that she used to wear kid gloves in bed. Edie Boardman said that to Bertha Supple once when she was black out with Gertie (the girl chums had of course their little tiffs like the rest of mortals) and told her not to let on whatever she did that it was her that told her or she'd never speak to her again, never. No, Honour where honour is due there was an innate refinement a languid queenly hauteur about Gertie which was unmistakably evidenced in her delicate hands and high arched instep. Had kind fate willed her to be born a gentlewoman of high degree in her own right and had she only received the benefit of a good education Gertie MacDowell might easily have held her own beside any lady of the land and have seen herself exquisitely gowned with jewels on her brow with aristocratic suitors at her feet vying with one another to pay their devoirs to her. Mayhap it was this (the love that might have been) that gave to her softly featured face at times a look, tense with suppressed meaning, and that imparted a strange yearning tenderness to the beautiful eyes. Why have women such eyes of witchery? Gertie's were of the deepest Irish blue set off by lustrous lashes and dark expressive brows. Time had been when those brows had not been so silkily seductive.
